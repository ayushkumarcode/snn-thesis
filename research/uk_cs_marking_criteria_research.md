# UK Computer Science Undergraduate Final Year Project: Marking Criteria Research

## Executive Summary

This report synthesises marking criteria, rubrics, and assessment practices from across Russell Group and other leading UK universities for undergraduate Computer Science final year projects (dissertations). The research draws on publicly available marking schemes from Cambridge, Edinburgh, Imperial, Bristol, Sussex, Warwick, Cardiff, Glasgow, Manchester (including your own COMP30040 documents), and general UK academic guidance.

The central findings are: (1) The written report/dissertation dominates assessment, typically carrying 55-80% of the total mark. (2) Implementation/achievements carry significant but secondary weight at 25-45%. (3) Evaluation quality is consistently cited as the differentiator between good and excellent projects. (4) Publishable results are categorically NOT expected from undergraduates -- they are treated as evidence of exceptional (80+) work only. (5) A well-framed research question matters enormously because it shapes the entire narrative of the report, but implementation quality carries more raw marks in most schemes.

## 1. PERCENTAGE MARK ALLOCATION BY CATEGORY

### 1.1 Directly Comparable University Schemes

The table below shows how different universities weight their assessment components. Note that universities structure their assessment differently -- some mark the report holistically, while others break out sub-components.

| University | Report / Dissertation | Implementation / Achievements | Presentation / Viva / Screencast | Other |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Manchester (COMP30040)** | 55% | 30% | 15% (screencast) | Q&A not directly assessed |
| **Cambridge (Part II)** | 100% (dissertation only, but broken into sub-categories below) | -- | -- | -- |
| **Warwick (CS310)** | 75% (final report + viva combined) | Embedded in report mark | 15% (progress report) | 10% (project management) |
| **Exeter (ECM3401)** | ~90% (report + presentation) | Embedded in report mark | Embedded | 10% (supervisor report) |
| **Imperial (MSc, indicative of UG approach)** | 85% (report) | Embedded in report mark | 15% (poster/presentation) | -- |

### 1.2 Cambridge Part II -- The Most Transparent Sub-Category Breakdown

Cambridge provides the most granular publicly available breakdown of what markers assess within the dissertation itself. The dissertation is marked out of 100 with these sub-categories:

| Category | Marks (out of 100) | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Introduction and Preparation** | 30 | Problem statement, literature review, project planning, background research, understanding of related work |
| **Implementation and Contribution** | 45 | Technical work, design decisions, code quality, system architecture, the actual "doing" of the project |
| **Evaluation and Conclusions** | 25 | Testing, benchmarking, critical analysis of results, reflection on achievements, future work |

Source: [Cambridge CST Assessment](https://www.cst.cam.ac.uk/teaching/part-ii/projects/assessment)

### 1.3 Edinburgh Informatics -- Tiered Criteria System

Edinburgh uses a qualitative rather than percentage-based approach, with three tiers:

**Basic Criteria** (must be satisfied for a pass):
- Understanding of the problem
- Completion of the work
- Quality of the work
- Quality of the dissertation

**Additional Criteria** (needed for good marks, 60+):
- Knowledge of the literature
- Critical evaluation of previous work
- Critical evaluation of own work
- Justification of design decisions
- Solution of conceptual problems
- Amount of work

**Exceptional Criteria** (needed for 80+):
- Evidence of outstanding merit, e.g. originality
- Inclusion of material worthy of publication
