\begin{abstract}
Spiking neural networks (SNNs) promise energy-efficient inference on neuromorphic hardware, yet no prior work has evaluated them on ESC-50---a challenging 50-class environmental sound benchmark.
We present the first such evaluation, comparing seven spike encodings and introducing a Rhythm SNN with learnable oscillatory threshold modulation that achieves 61.10\% accuracy---only 2.75~pp below a matched ANN baseline (63.85\%)---while consuming 3.3$\times$ less energy per inference (142 vs 463~nJ, NeuroBench).
Weight pruning at 85\% sparsity reduces energy a further 3.5$\times$, yielding an estimated \textbf{11$\times$ energy advantage} over the ANN with only 1.5~pp accuracy loss.
We validate the pruned models on SpiNNaker neuromorphic hardware, running 22{,}000+ inferences across 10 sparsity levels.
Pruning acts as implicit regularization: at 50\% sparsity, hardware accuracy \emph{improves} by 3.1~pp, and at two sparsity levels SpiNNaker exceeds its own software simulation.
These results establish the first energy-accuracy Pareto frontier for spiking audio classification and demonstrate that SNNs can match ANN accuracy at a fraction of the energy cost.
Code: \url{https://github.com/ayushkumarcode/snn-thesis}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Environmental sound classification has practical applications in smart cities, wildlife monitoring, and assistive technology, but deploying classifiers on battery-constrained edge devices remains an energy bottleneck.
Spiking neural networks (SNNs) offer a path forward: on neuromorphic hardware, each accumulate operation (AC) costs 5.1$\times$ less than a multiply-accumulate (MAC)~\cite{horowitz2014}, and inactive synapses consume zero power---an advantage that conventional GPUs cannot exploit.
Yet SNNs are widely assumed to sacrifice accuracy for efficiency.
We show this trade-off is not inevitable: a Rhythm SNN with learnable oscillatory thresholds achieves 61.10\% on ESC-50---only 2.75~pp below the matched ANN---at 3.3$\times$ lower energy per inference.
With 85\% weight pruning, this advantage grows to an estimated \textbf{11$\times$}, validated on SpiNNaker neuromorphic hardware.

Despite growing interest in neuromorphic computing~\cite{schuman2022,maass1997}, \textbf{no prior work has evaluated convolutional SNNs on ESC-50}---a 50-class benchmark with 2{,}000 recordings that remains the standard for environmental sound research~\cite{piczak2015}.
The closest SNN work classifies only ESC-10 (10 classes) with FC layers~\cite{larroza2025}; Dominguez-Morales et al.~\cite{dominguez2016} deploy SNNs on SpiNNaker for audio but evaluate only pure tones.
No study has established an energy--accuracy Pareto frontier for spiking audio classification.

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First SNN evaluation on ESC-50} with seven spike encodings and a Rhythm SNN that narrows the ANN gap to 2.75~pp;
    \item \textbf{11$\times$ energy reduction} versus a matched ANN through reduced timesteps ($T$=3) and 85\% weight pruning, benchmarked with NeuroBench~\cite{yik2025};
    \item \textbf{Pruning as implicit regularization}: 50\% sparsity improves neuromorphic hardware accuracy by 3.1~pp, validated across 22{,}000+ SpiNNaker inferences;
    \item \textbf{First neuromorphic deployment for environmental sound}, with root-cause analysis of deployment failure modes and a validated hybrid approach on SpiNNaker.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%% ============================================================

\textbf{ESC-50}~\cite{piczak2015} contains 2{,}000 five-second recordings across 50 classes in five super-categories.
Human performance is 81.3\%; ANN state of the art reaches 99\%+ with large pretrained models~\cite{omnivec2}.
We use log-mel spectrograms (64 bins, $n_\text{fft}$=1024, hop=512, sr=22050\,Hz), yielding 64$\times$216 inputs.

\textbf{Spiking Neural Networks}~\cite{maass1997}.
LIF neurons accumulate weighted input and spike at threshold:
$U[t] = \beta \cdot U[t{-}1] + W \cdot X[t] - S[t{-}1] \cdot U_\text{thr}$,
where $\beta$=0.95, and $S[t]$=1 if $U[t] > U_\text{thr}$.
The non-differentiable step uses a fast-sigmoid surrogate gradient~\cite{neftci2019,zenke2021} (slope=25).

\textbf{SpiNNaker}~\cite{furber2014} is a million-core neuromorphic platform with integer-quantized weights and spike-driven communication.
We use IF\_curr\_exp neurons ($\tau_\text{syn}$=5\,ms, $v_\text{thresh}$=1.0, $\tau_m$=20\,ms) on a SpiNN-5 board.
Prior SpiNNaker classification work includes spiking DBNs achieving 95\% on MNIST~\cite{stromatias2015} and deep spiking ConvNets~\cite{patino2020}.
Recent work deploys gesture recognition on SpiNNaker~2~\cite{arfa2025}; we target the original SpiNNaker with environmental sound---a harder, multi-class task.

\textbf{Prior SNN audio work} is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}.
Yarga et al.~\cite{yarga2022} compare four spike encodings for speech digits at ICONS~2022; we extend to seven encodings on a harder 50-class task.
Seekings et al.~\cite{seekings2024} deploy hybrid SNNs on Loihi and Jetson at ICONS~2024, motivating our hybrid SpiNNaker approach.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Architecture}

Both SNN and ANN share an identical $\sim$622K-parameter architecture (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).
The network consists of two convolutional blocks (Conv2d$\to$BN$\to$MaxPool(2)$\to$LIF), followed by AvgPool(4,6) and two FC layers (FC(2304,256)$\to$LIF$\to$FC(256,50)$\to$LIF).
The ANN replaces each LIF with ReLU.
Training uses Adam (lr=$10^{-3}$, wd=$10^{-4}$), ReduceLROnPlateau, early stopping (patience=10), batch size 32, standard 5-fold CV on ESC-50's predefined fold structure.
The SNN uses cross-entropy loss on summed output membrane potentials across all $T$ timesteps (rate decoding).
All experiments run on NVIDIA A100 GPUs (CSF3 HPC, University of Manchester).

\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Spike Encoding Methods}

We evaluate seven encoding methods (Table~\ref{tab:encodings}), the most comprehensive comparison for audio SNNs to date---extending Yarga et al.'s four-encoding comparison~\cite{yarga2022} to seven methods on a harder 50-class task.
Encodings convert the 64$\times$216 spectrogram into spike trains across $T$ timesteps.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Seven spike encoding methods evaluated. Encodings convert spectrogram pixels to spike trains across $T$=25 timesteps.}
\label{tab:encodings}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Encoding & Description & Spikes/neuron \\
\midrule
Rate & Prob.\ $\propto$ intensity & $\sim$7 \\
Latency & Early spike = high intensity & 1 \\
Delta & Spikes on intensity changes & Variable \\
Direct & Raw values repeated $T$ times & N/A (continuous) \\
Burst & Front-loaded, $\propto$ intensity & $\sim$5 \\
Phase & Time in oscillation cycle & 1 (deterministic) \\
Population & 10 neurons/class, MSE loss & Variable \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Direct encoding} bypasses discretization entirely, feeding continuous spectrogram values at every timestep.
The LIF neuron's membrane dynamics provide internal temporal integration---effectively learning its own spike timing from continuous input.
\textbf{Rate encoding} converts each pixel intensity to a spike probability per timestep (stochastic; $\sim$7 spikes/neuron on average).
\textbf{Phase encoding} maps pixel intensity to a spike time within one oscillation cycle (deterministic; exactly 1 spike/neuron).
\textbf{Latency encoding} maps high intensity to earlier spike times (1 spike/neuron, temporal precision).
\textbf{Delta encoding} emits spikes on intensity changes between timesteps---designed for event-driven sensors, ineffective for static spectrograms.
\textbf{Burst encoding} concentrates spikes proportional to intensity in the first 5 timesteps.
\textbf{Population encoding} assigns 10 output neurons per class (500 total) with MSE loss on spike counts.

\subsection{Rhythm SNN}

We augment the LIF neuron with learnable oscillatory threshold modulation:
\begin{equation}
U_\text{thr}(t) = U_\text{thr}^0 + A \sin(2\pi f t + \phi)
\label{eq:rhythm}
\end{equation}
where amplitude $A$, frequency $f$, and phase $\phi$ are learnable per-layer parameters (total: 2{,}010 additional params, 0.3\% overhead).
Biological auditory neurons exhibit oscillatory excitability that gates temporal attention---our Rhythm SNN is a simplified computational analogue.
The modulated threshold creates alternating windows of high and low excitability across timesteps, allowing different neurons to specialize on different temporal features within the spectrogram.
We train with $T$=3 timesteps (reduced from $T$=25 baseline) using spike-rate-escape surrogate gradient~\cite{zenke2021}, forcing the network to extract information efficiently from very few spikes.

\subsection{Pruning with Mask Enforcement}

We apply unstructured magnitude pruning at 10 sparsity levels (50--95\%) to FC$_2$ weights before SpiNNaker deployment.
At each level, weights below the magnitude threshold are zeroed and a binary mask is enforced during the SpiNNaker weight conversion:
\begin{equation}
W_\text{SpiNN} = \text{round}(W \odot M \cdot s)
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the binary pruning mask, $s$ is the calibrated weight scale, and $\text{round}(\cdot)$ maps to SpiNNaker integer weights.
Pruning reduces the number of non-zero synaptic connections, directly lowering both the number of synaptic events on SpiNNaker and the NeuroBench effective ACs in simulation.

\subsection{SpiNNaker Deployment}

Full SNN deployment on SpiNNaker fails because AvgPool produces fractional outputs: FC$_1$ weights have near-zero mean ($\mu$=$-$0.0034), so 2{,}304 fractional inputs cause excitatory-inhibitory cancellation, producing zero hidden spikes (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}).
We adopt a hybrid approach: conv layers and FC$_1$+LIF$_3$ run in software, producing binary hidden spikes (256-d, $\sim$22\% active per step).
Only FC$_2$ (256$\to$50)+LIF$_4$ runs on SpiNNaker via current injection, using the sPyNNaker software stack~\cite{rhodes2018}.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/spinnaker_pipeline.pdf}
\caption{Hybrid deployment pipeline. Full deployment failed due to AvgPool producing fractional outputs. Our validated approach runs Conv+FC$_1$ in software and deploys only FC$_2$ on SpiNNaker.}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

We deploy each pruned model across all 5 folds (400 samples per fold = 2{,}000 inferences per pruning level, 10 levels + unpruned = 22{,}000 total hardware inferences).
Critical implementation details: (1)~explicit membrane initialization (\texttt{v=0.0}), as sPyNNaker defaults to $v$=$-$65\,mV; (2)~core splitting (32 neurons/core) to prevent DMA overflows; (3)~batched reset between samples to clear residual membrane state.

\subsection{Energy Analysis}

We count effective ACs (SNN) and MACs (ANN) following NeuroBench~\cite{yik2025}, with energy constants from Horowitz ISSCC 2014~\cite{horowitz2014}: AC=0.9\,pJ, MAC=4.6\,pJ.
Each pruning level is evaluated across all 5 folds for robust energy estimates.

Table~\ref{tab:encoding_results} presents the first systematic encoding comparison for audio SNNs on a 50-class task.

A cross-encoding transfer analysis (models evaluated with mismatched encodings) yields a transfer ratio of 0.255$\pm$0.006---swapping encodings at test time causes 75\% accuracy loss, demonstrating that SNNs learn highly encoding-specific representations.
To our knowledge, this is the first quantification of encoding specificity in SNNs.

\subsection{Rhythm SNN Improvement}

The Rhythm SNN (Eq.~\ref{eq:rhythm}) with $T$=3 timesteps achieves 61.10\%$\pm$1.99\% (fold accuracies: 61.0, 62.25, 62.0, 66.25, 55.25)---a 13.95~pp improvement over the baseline SNN (47.15\%, $T$=25) and only 2.75~pp below the ANN (63.85\%).
The learned oscillation parameters converge to biologically plausible values: frequencies of 2.1--2.9 (corresponding to theta-range oscillations) and amplitudes of 0.03--0.15 (3--15\% threshold modulation).
Combining with stochastic resonance noise ($\sigma$=0.02) yields 61.35\%$\pm$3.54\%.
The improvement comes from both the oscillatory modulation and the reduced timestep count ($T$=3 vs $T$=25), which forces the network to make efficient use of each spike.

Table~\ref{tab:pruned_spinnaker} presents the main result: a comprehensive sweep of pruned SNN deployment on SpiNNaker across 10 sparsity levels.
Three findings stand out:

\textbf{(1) Pruning as regularization.}
At 50\% sparsity, SpiNNaker accuracy \emph{increases} from 57.35\% to 60.45\% (+3.10~pp, paired $t$-test $p$=0.049 across 5 folds).
Crucially, snnTorch accuracy also improves (59.50\%$\to$61.85\%), ruling out reduced quantization error as the sole explanation---pruning acts as implicit regularization in \emph{both} software and hardware, removing redundant weights whose small magnitudes contribute noise under integer quantization.
The effect is remarkably persistent: SpiNNaker accuracy remains above 59\% from 50\% through 85\% sparsity, a plateau spanning 35 percentage points of weight removal.

\textbf{(2) SpiNNaker exceeds software simulation.}
At 60\% and 80\% pruning, SpiNNaker achieves \emph{higher} accuracy than snnTorch (gaps of $-$0.45~pp and $-$0.30~pp respectively).
We hypothesize this arises from stochastic rounding during integer weight conversion: at these sparsity levels, the remaining weights are large-magnitude and the rounding noise acts as beneficial regularization, similar to stochastic quantization.

\textbf{(3) Graceful degradation.}
Accuracy remains above 54\% even at 95\% sparsity (only 5\% of weights retained), demonstrating the FC$_2$ classifier is highly over-parameterized for this task.
The hardware gap stays below 2~pp across all levels except 95\%.

Under FGSM~\cite{goodfellow2015} at $\varepsilon$=0.1, the SNN retains 16.55\% accuracy versus 2.75\% for the ANN (\textbf{6.0$\times$ more robust}, $p$=0.007, 5-fold validated; Table~\ref{tab:adversarial}).

This has practical implications: in a hybrid deployment, CNN14 extracts features in software while the SNN head classifies on neuromorphic hardware with $<$1~pp accuracy loss.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\textbf{Why does pruning improve hardware accuracy?}
We identify two complementary mechanisms.
First, pruning removes small-magnitude weights that are most susceptible to integer quantization error.
With fewer weights, the surviving connections have larger magnitudes that quantize more faithfully.
Second, the sparser weight matrix produces cleaner spike patterns: fewer simultaneous synaptic inputs reduce the chance of excitatory-inhibitory cancellation that plagued the full (unpruned) deployment.
This ``pruning as regularization'' effect is consistent with lottery ticket hypothesis findings~\cite{eshraghian2023} but novel in the context of neuromorphic hardware deployment.

\textbf{Negative hardware gaps.}
At 60\% and 80\% pruning, SpiNNaker \emph{exceeds} snnTorch.
We attribute this to beneficial noise injection from integer weight rounding at specific sparsity levels where the weight magnitude distribution interacts favorably with the quantization grid.
This is analogous to stochastic resonance---a phenomenon we verified independently in our SNN (accuracy improves by +0.25~pp with $\sigma$=0.02 noise).

\textbf{FC$_1$ failure as co-design insight.}
Standard conv$\to$AvgPool$\to$FC architectures are not directly deployable on spike-only hardware.
AvgPool produces fractional outputs; FC$_1$ weights with near-zero mean cause cancellation.
The solution---replacing AvgPool with MaxPool---preserves binary outputs and enables full deployment (verified on fold~4: 43.75\% with threshold=3.0).
Full MaxPool-retrained SpiNNaker deployment is future work; our FC$_2$-only hybrid demonstrates the pipeline and quantifies the gap.

\textbf{Practical deployment path.}
In a real system, CNN14 extracts features in software (92.50\% accuracy), then the pruned SNN head classifies on SpiNNaker ($<$1~pp loss, 3.5$\times$ energy reduction).
This hybrid paradigm---heavy feature extraction in software, lightweight spiking classification on neuromorphic hardware---is the emerging standard~\cite{seekings2024}.

\textbf{Connection to the lottery ticket hypothesis.}
Frankle~\&~Carlin~\cite{frankle2019} showed that dense networks contain sparse subnetworks (``winning tickets'') that train to full accuracy.
Our finding extends this to the hardware deployment setting: the pruned FC$_2$ subnetwork is not merely as good as the dense network on SpiNNaker---it is \emph{better}, because the surviving high-magnitude weights quantize more faithfully to SpiNNaker's integer representation.
The ``winning ticket'' for neuromorphic hardware is the subnetwork whose weight distribution is best aligned with the target quantization grid.
This suggests that hardware-aware pruning criteria (e.g., maximizing post-quantization fidelity rather than minimizing magnitude) could further improve deployment accuracy.

\textbf{The gap is feature learning, not spiking.}
The PANNs result (Table~\ref{tab:panns}) confirms: given AudioSet-pretrained features, SNN~$\approx$~ANN (0.95~pp, $p$=0.034).
The 16.7~pp scratch gap arises from learning convolutional filters from only 1{,}600 samples with approximate surrogate gradients and binary spike bottlenecks~\cite{deng2020}---not from a fundamental limitation of spiking computation.

\textbf{Limitations.}
(1)~Only FC$_2$ runs on SpiNNaker; full deployment requires replacing AvgPool with MaxPool and retraining (verified feasible: 43.75\% on fold~4 with threshold=3.0).
(2)~Energy is estimated via NeuroBench operation counting, not measured hardware power; actual SpiNNaker power measurement requires on-board metering not available through remote access.
(3)~ESC-50 is small (2{,}000 clips); generalization to UrbanSound8K or FSD50K is untested.
(4)~Adversarial evaluation uses standard PGD; SA-PGD~\cite{wang2025} may reveal different robustness profiles.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We present the first convolutional SNN evaluation on ESC-50, with a Rhythm SNN achieving 61.10\%---only 2.75~pp below the ANN---and deploy pruned models on SpiNNaker neuromorphic hardware.
The key finding is that \textbf{pruning acts as implicit regularization for hardware deployment}: 50\% sparsity improves SpiNNaker accuracy by 3.1~pp while reducing energy, and at 85\% sparsity the hardware gap is only 0.6~pp with 3.5$\times$ energy savings.
At two sparsity levels, SpiNNaker exceeds its own software simulation.
These 22{,}000 hardware inferences across 10 pruning levels constitute the most comprehensive neuromorphic audio deployment study to date.

Several directions emerge from this work.
First, SpiNNaker~2~\cite{hoppner2024} deployment with MaxPool-retrained architectures would enable full on-chip inference, eliminating the software feature-extraction step.
Second, structured pruning (removing entire neurons rather than individual weights) could exploit SpiNNaker's core-level parallelism for additional energy savings.
Third, cross-dataset validation on UrbanSound8K and FSD50K would test generalization beyond ESC-50.
Fourth, combining pruning with the PANNs+SNN hybrid (92.50\% accuracy) could yield a high-accuracy, energy-efficient system suitable for real-world always-on audio monitoring.
Finally, measuring actual SpiNNaker power consumption (rather than NeuroBench estimates) would provide ground-truth energy numbers for the pruned deployment pipeline.
