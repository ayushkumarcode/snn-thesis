\begin{abstract}
Spiking neural networks (SNNs) promise energy-efficient inference on neuromorphic hardware, yet no prior work has evaluated them on ESC-50---a challenging 50-class environmental sound benchmark.
We present the first such evaluation, comparing seven spike encodings and introducing a Rhythm SNN with learnable oscillatory threshold modulation that achieves 61.10\% accuracy---only 2.75~pp below a matched ANN baseline (63.85\%)---while consuming 3.2$\times$ less energy per inference (142 vs 454~nJ, NeuroBench).
At $T$=1, energy drops to just 47~nJ (\textbf{9.6$\times$ cheaper than ANN}), and weight pruning at 85\% sparsity compounds the advantage further.
We validate the pruned models on SpiNNaker neuromorphic hardware, running 22{,}000+ inferences across 10 sparsity levels.
Pruning acts as implicit regularization: at 50\% sparsity, hardware accuracy \emph{improves} by 3.1~pp, and at two sparsity levels SpiNNaker exceeds its own software simulation.
With pretrained audio features, the SNN--ANN gap collapses to 0.95~pp (92.50\% vs 93.45\%), confirming the scratch-training gap is a feature-learning limitation, not a fundamental constraint of spiking computation.
Code: \url{https://github.com/ayushkumarcode/snn-thesis}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

A single inference on ESC-50 costs 454~nJ with a conventional neural network.
We show a pruned spiking network can classify the same 50 environmental sounds for as little as 41~nJ---while deploying on real neuromorphic hardware with under 1~percentage point accuracy loss.
Spiking neural networks (SNNs) offer a path forward: on neuromorphic hardware, each accumulate operation (AC) costs 5.1$\times$ less than a multiply-accumulate (MAC)~\cite{horowitz2014}, and inactive synapses consume zero power---an advantage that conventional GPUs cannot exploit.
Yet SNNs are widely assumed to sacrifice accuracy for efficiency.
We show this trade-off is not inevitable: a Rhythm SNN with learnable oscillatory thresholds achieves 61.10\% on ESC-50---only 2.75~pp below the matched ANN---while the single-timestep variant consumes just 47~nJ per inference (\textbf{9.6$\times$ cheaper}).
Both configurations deploy successfully on SpiNNaker neuromorphic hardware with $<$4~pp accuracy loss.

Despite growing interest in neuromorphic computing~\cite{schuman2022,maass1997}, \textbf{no prior work has evaluated convolutional SNNs on ESC-50}---a 50-class benchmark with 2{,}000 recordings that remains the standard for environmental sound research~\cite{piczak2015}.
The closest SNN work classifies only ESC-10 (10 classes) with FC layers~\cite{larroza2025}; Dominguez-Morales et al.~\cite{dominguez2016} deploy SNNs on SpiNNaker for audio but evaluate only pure tones.
No study has established an energy--accuracy Pareto frontier for spiking audio classification.

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{First SNN evaluation on ESC-50} with seven spike encodings and a Rhythm SNN that narrows the ANN gap to 2.75~pp;
    \item \textbf{Up to 9.6$\times$ energy reduction} versus a matched ANN ($T$=1: 47~nJ vs 454~nJ), with weight pruning compounding the advantage further, benchmarked with NeuroBench~\cite{yik2025};
    \item \textbf{Pruning as implicit regularization}: 50\% sparsity improves neuromorphic hardware accuracy by 3.1~pp, validated across 22{,}000+ SpiNNaker inferences;
    \item \textbf{First neuromorphic deployment for multi-class environmental sound} (beyond pure tones~\cite{dominguez2016}), with root-cause analysis of deployment failure modes and a validated hybrid approach on SpiNNaker.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
%% ============================================================

\textbf{ESC-50}~\cite{piczak2015} contains 2{,}000 five-second recordings across 50 classes organized in five super-categories (Animals, Nature, Human non-speech, Domestic, Urban), each with 40 clips.
The dataset provides 5 predefined folds for cross-validation, ensuring no recording or source overlap between train and test splits.
Human performance is 81.3\%; ANN state of the art exceeds 99\% with large pretrained transformers~\cite{omnivec2}, but these models require billions of MACs per inference.
We use log-mel spectrograms (64 bins, $n_\text{fft}$=1024, hop=512, sr=22050\,Hz), yielding 64$\times$216 input tensors normalized to $[0,1]$.
The small training set (1{,}600 samples per fold) makes ESC-50 particularly challenging for SNNs, which must learn discriminative features through approximate surrogate gradients.

\textbf{Spiking Neural Networks}~\cite{maass1997}.
LIF neurons accumulate weighted input and spike at threshold:
$U[t] = \beta \cdot U[t{-}1] + W \cdot X[t] - S[t{-}1] \cdot U_\text{thr}$,
where $\beta$=0.95, and $S[t]$=1 if $U[t] > U_\text{thr}$.
The non-differentiable step uses a fast-sigmoid surrogate gradient~\cite{neftci2019,zenke2021} (slope=25).

\textbf{SpiNNaker}~\cite{furber2014} is a million-core neuromorphic platform with integer-quantized weights and spike-driven communication.
We use IF\_curr\_exp neurons ($\tau_\text{syn}$=5\,ms, $v_\text{thresh}$=1.0, $\tau_m$=20\,ms) on a SpiNN-5 board.
Prior SpiNNaker classification work includes spiking DBNs achieving 95\% on MNIST~\cite{stromatias2015} and deep spiking ConvNets~\cite{patino2020}.
Recent work deploys gesture recognition on SpiNNaker~2~\cite{arfa2025}; we target the original SpiNNaker with environmental sound---a harder, multi-class task.

\textbf{Prior SNN audio work} is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}.
Yarga et al.~\cite{yarga2022} compare four spike encodings for speech digits at ICONS~2022; we extend to seven encodings on a harder 50-class task.
Seekings et al.~\cite{seekings2024} deploy hybrid SNNs on Loihi and Jetson at ICONS~2024, motivating our hybrid SpiNNaker approach.

\subsection{Related Work}

\textbf{SNN audio classification.}
Only three prior studies apply SNNs to environmental sound; none use convolutional architectures or exceed 10 classes (Table~\ref{tab:comparison}).
Larroza et al.~\cite{larroza2025} recently evaluated three spike encodings on ESC-10 (10 classes), achieving 69\% with Send-on-Delta encoding using FC layers only.
Yarga et al.~\cite{yarga2022} compared four encodings for speech digits at ICONS~2022, demonstrating that encoding choice significantly impacts SNN accuracy.
We extend both works: seven encodings on ESC-50 (50 classes), with convolutional architectures, hardware deployment, and energy analysis.

\textbf{Neuromorphic hardware deployment.}
SpiNNaker~\cite{furber2014} deployment for classification includes spiking deep belief networks (95\% MNIST~\cite{stromatias2015}) and deep convolutional SNNs (98.2\% MNIST~\cite{patino2020}).
Seekings et al.~\cite{seekings2024} deploy hybrid SNNs on Loihi and Jetson at ICONS~2024; Arfa et al.~\cite{arfa2025} fine-tune spiking Q-networks on SpiNNaker~2 with 32$\times$ energy reduction.
All target vision or control tasks---\textbf{no neuromorphic audio classification exists beyond pure tones}~\cite{dominguez2016}.

\textbf{SNN energy efficiency.}
Dampfhoffer et al.~\cite{dampfhoffer2023} show SNNs need $<$6.4\% spike rate to beat quantized ANNs on conventional hardware.
The NeuroBench framework~\cite{yik2025} standardizes energy comparison via synaptic operation counting.
Our work contributes the first energy--accuracy Pareto analysis for spiking audio, showing that pruning and temporal reduction together yield significant energy advantages.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Architecture}

Both SNN and ANN share an identical $\sim$622K-parameter architecture (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).
The network consists of two convolutional blocks (Conv2d$\to$BN$\to$MaxPool(2)$\to$LIF), followed by AvgPool(4,6) and two FC layers (FC(2304,256)$\to$LIF$\to$FC(256,50)$\to$LIF).
The ANN replaces each LIF with ReLU.
Training uses Adam (lr=$10^{-3}$, wd=$10^{-4}$), ReduceLROnPlateau, early stopping (patience=10), batch size 32, standard 5-fold CV on ESC-50's predefined fold structure.
The SNN uses cross-entropy loss on summed output membrane potentials across all $T$ timesteps (rate decoding).
All experiments run on NVIDIA A100 GPUs (CSF3 HPC, University of Manchester).

\subsection{Spike Encoding Methods}

We evaluate seven encoding methods (Table~\ref{tab:encodings}), extending Yarga et al.'s four-encoding comparison~\cite{yarga2022} with three additional methods (delta, burst, population) on a 50-class task.
Encodings convert the 64$\times$216 spectrogram into spike trains across $T$ timesteps.

\begin{table}[t]
\caption{Seven spike encoding methods evaluated. Encodings convert spectrogram pixels to spike trains across $T$=25 timesteps.}
\label{tab:encodings}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Encoding & Description & Spikes/neuron \\
\midrule
Rate & Prob.\ $\propto$ intensity & $\sim$7 \\
Latency & Early spike = high intensity & 1 \\
Delta & Spikes on intensity changes & Variable \\
Direct & Raw values repeated $T$ times & N/A (continuous) \\
Burst & Front-loaded, $\propto$ intensity & $\sim$5 \\
Phase & Time in oscillation cycle & 1 (deterministic) \\
Population & 10 neurons/class, MSE loss & Variable \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Direct encoding} bypasses discretization entirely, feeding continuous spectrogram values at every timestep.
The LIF neuron's membrane dynamics provide internal temporal integration---effectively learning its own spike timing from continuous input.
\textbf{Rate encoding} converts each pixel intensity to a spike probability per timestep (stochastic; $\sim$7 spikes/neuron on average).
\textbf{Phase encoding} maps pixel intensity to a spike time within one oscillation cycle (deterministic; exactly 1 spike/neuron).
\textbf{Latency encoding} maps high intensity to earlier spike times (1 spike/neuron, temporal precision).
\textbf{Delta encoding} emits spikes on intensity changes between timesteps---designed for event-driven sensors, ineffective for static spectrograms.
\textbf{Burst encoding} concentrates spikes proportional to intensity in the first 5 timesteps.
\textbf{Population encoding} assigns 10 output neurons per class (500 total) with MSE loss on spike counts.

\subsection{Rhythm SNN}

We augment the LIF neuron with learnable oscillatory threshold modulation:
\begin{equation}
U_\text{thr}(t) = U_\text{thr}^0 + A \sin(2\pi f t + \phi)
\label{eq:rhythm}
\end{equation}
where amplitude $A$, frequency $f$, and phase $\phi$ are learnable per-layer parameters (total: 1{,}608 additional params, 0.3\% overhead).
Biological auditory neurons exhibit oscillatory excitability that gates temporal attention---our Rhythm SNN is a simplified computational analogue.
The modulated threshold creates alternating windows of high and low excitability across timesteps, allowing different neurons to specialize on different temporal features within the spectrogram.
We train with $T$=3 timesteps (reduced from $T$=25 baseline) using spike-rate-escape surrogate gradient~\cite{zenke2021}, forcing the network to extract information efficiently from very few spikes.

\subsection{Pruning with Mask Enforcement}

We apply unstructured magnitude pruning at 10 sparsity levels (50--95\%) to FC$_2$ weights before SpiNNaker deployment.
At each level, weights below the magnitude threshold are zeroed and a binary mask is enforced during the SpiNNaker weight conversion:
\begin{equation}
W_\text{SpiNN} = \text{round}(W \odot M \cdot s)
\end{equation}
where $M$ is the binary pruning mask, $s$ is the calibrated weight scale, and $\text{round}(\cdot)$ maps to SpiNNaker integer weights.
Pruning reduces the number of non-zero synaptic connections, directly lowering both the number of synaptic events on SpiNNaker and the NeuroBench effective ACs in simulation.

\subsection{SpiNNaker Deployment}

Full SNN deployment on SpiNNaker fails because AvgPool produces fractional (non-binary) outputs.
FC$_1$ weights have near-zero mean ($\mu$=$-$0.0034), so 2{,}304 fractional inputs cause excitatory-inhibitory cancellation, yielding zero hidden spikes (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}).
We adopt a hybrid approach: convolutional feature extraction runs on the host CPU, which pre-computes FC$_1$ input currents.
These currents are injected into 256 IF\_curr\_exp neurons on SpiNNaker, where LIF$_3$ integrate-and-fire dynamics produce binary hidden spikes entirely on neuromorphic hardware.
FC$_2$ (256$\to$50 synaptic weights + 50 output neurons) also runs on SpiNNaker via the sPyNNaker stack~\cite{rhodes2018}.
In total, \textbf{306 spiking neurons and all classification-stage synapses execute on SpiNNaker}; only the convolutional feature extraction remains on the host.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/spinnaker_pipeline.pdf}
\caption{Hybrid deployment pipeline. Full deployment failed due to AvgPool producing fractional outputs. Our validated approach runs Conv+FC$_1$ in software and deploys only FC$_2$ on SpiNNaker.}
\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure}

We deploy each pruned model across all 5 folds (400 samples per fold = 2{,}000 inferences per pruning level, 10 levels + unpruned = 22{,}000 total hardware inferences).

\textbf{Weight conversion.}
snnTorch float32 weights are multiplied by a calibrated scale factor $s$ and rounded to SpiNNaker integers.
The scale $s$ is determined by a 9-point logarithmic sweep on a single correctly-classified sample: we select the scale that maximizes hidden neuron activity while preserving correct classification.
Positive weights map to excitatory synapses, negative to inhibitory; SpiNNaker's IF\_curr\_exp model requires separate excitatory and inhibitory projections.

\textbf{Implementation pitfalls.}
Three issues required debugging.
First, sPyNNaker defaults membrane potential to $v$=$-$65\,mV regardless of the configured rest potential, so explicit initialization to $v$=0 is required---without this, neurons need 66\,mV of input to fire.
Second, high-fan-in layers (256 inputs) overflow SpiNNaker's DMA buffers unless cores are split to 32 neurons per core.
Third, membrane state must be explicitly reset between samples to prevent temporal bleed between classifications.

\subsection{Energy Analysis}

We follow the NeuroBench framework~\cite{yik2025} to count effective synaptic operations: ACs for the SNN (spike-gated, only non-zero when the presynaptic neuron fires) and MACs for the ANN (dense, all weights active every forward pass).
Energy constants are from Horowitz ISSCC 2014~\cite{horowitz2014} at the 45\,nm node: AC=0.9\,pJ, MAC=4.6\,pJ---a 5.1$\times$ per-operation advantage for spike-driven computation.
The SNN's energy depends on two factors: the number of timesteps $T$ (more steps = more operations) and the spike rate (sparser firing = fewer ACs).
Our Rhythm SNN at $T$=3 fires at $\sim$31\% spike rate, meaning 69\% of synapses are inactive each step.
Each pruning level is evaluated across all 5 folds for robust energy estimates.

Table~\ref{tab:encoding_results} presents the first systematic encoding comparison for audio SNNs on a 50-class task.
Direct encoding outperforms all spike-converted methods by $>$20~pp (Figure~\ref{fig:encodings}).
Phase (24.15\%) and rate (24.00\%) achieve statistically identical accuracy ($p$=0.93), despite phase producing exactly one spike per neuron versus rate's $\sim$7---confirming that temporal window coverage, not spike count, determines accuracy.
Delta (7.25\%) fails because mel spectrograms are static images---delta encoding is designed for event-driven sensors (e.g., DVS cameras) where intensity \emph{changes} are the signal; applying it to a fixed spectrogram produces near-zero spike activity.
Burst encoding (6.50\%) concentrates all spikes in the first 5 of 25 timesteps, creating a severe temporal mismatch: the LIF membrane integrates over the full window, but information arrives only in the first 20\%, causing extreme overfitting (train 45--62\%, test 5--9\%).
Latency encoding (16.30\%) produces only one spike per neuron, providing insufficient temporal drive for the LIF dynamics to discriminate between 50 classes.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/encoding_bar_chart.pdf}

A cross-encoding transfer analysis (models trained with one encoding, tested with each of the other six) yields a transfer ratio of 0.255$\pm$0.006 with remarkably low fold-to-fold variation ($<$2.4\% coefficient of variation)---swapping encodings at test time causes 75\% accuracy loss on average.
This demonstrates that SNNs learn highly encoding-specific internal representations, with each encoding inducing a distinct weight structure.
To our knowledge, this is the first quantification of encoding specificity in SNNs, and has practical implications for neuromorphic system design: the encoding method must be fixed at design time, as models cannot be repurposed for different sensor front-ends without retraining.

\subsection{Rhythm SNN Improvement}

The Rhythm SNN (Eq.~\ref{eq:rhythm}) with $T$=3 timesteps achieves 61.10\%$\pm$1.99\% (fold accuracies: 60.25, 63.50, 61.50, 62.50, 57.75)---a 13.95~pp improvement over the baseline SNN (47.15\%, $T$=25) and only 2.75~pp below the ANN (63.85\%).
The learned oscillation parameters converge to frequencies of 2.1--2.9 (delta-range, consistent with auditory cortex slow oscillations) and amplitudes of 0.03--0.15 (3--15\% threshold modulation).
Combining with stochastic resonance noise ($\sigma$=0.02) yields 61.35\%$\pm$3.54\%.
The improvement comes from both the oscillatory modulation and the reduced timestep count ($T$=3 vs $T$=25), which forces the network to extract information efficiently from each spike.

We conducted 34 experiments on A100 GPUs exploring combinations of techniques: stochastic resonance, dendritic processing, knowledge distillation, and cochleagram features.
Key findings: (1)~knowledge distillation consistently hurts SNN training, reducing accuracy in every combination tested; (2)~cochleagram features (biologically-motivated filterbank) improve baseline accuracy from 47.15\% to 55.35\% (+8.2~pp); (3)~the Rhythm SNN with learned per-neuron $\beta$ values (decay constant) and spike-rate-escape surrogate achieves the best results.
The learned $\beta$ values converge to 0.93--0.95 across layers, close to the manually-set $\beta$=0.95, suggesting the default is near-optimal for this task.

Table~\ref{tab:pruned_spinnaker} presents the main result: a comprehensive sweep of pruned SNN deployment on SpiNNaker across 10 sparsity levels.
Three findings stand out:

\textbf{(1) Pruning as regularization.}
At 50\% sparsity, SpiNNaker accuracy \emph{increases} from 57.35\% to 60.45\% (+3.10~pp, paired $t$-test $p$=0.003 across 5 folds).
Crucially, snnTorch accuracy also improves (59.50\%$\to$61.85\%), ruling out reduced quantization error as the sole explanation---pruning acts as implicit regularization in \emph{both} software and hardware, removing redundant weights whose small magnitudes contribute noise under integer quantization.
The effect is remarkably persistent: SpiNNaker accuracy remains above 59\% from 50\% through 85\% sparsity, a plateau spanning 35 percentage points of weight removal.

\textbf{(2) SpiNNaker exceeds software simulation.}
At 60\% and 80\% pruning, SpiNNaker achieves \emph{higher} accuracy than snnTorch (gaps of $-$0.45~pp and $-$0.30~pp respectively).
We hypothesize this arises from stochastic rounding during integer weight conversion: at these sparsity levels, the remaining weights are large-magnitude and the rounding noise acts as beneficial regularization, similar to stochastic quantization.

\textbf{(3) Graceful degradation.}
Accuracy remains above 54\% even at 95\% sparsity (only 5\% of weights retained), demonstrating the FC$_2$ classifier is highly over-parameterized for this task.
The hardware gap stays below 2~pp across all levels except 95\%.

\textbf{(4) Timestep--hardware interaction.}
We deployed models at six timestep values ($T \in \{1,2,3,4,5,7\}$) on SpiNNaker (Table~\ref{tab:tsweep}).
Only $T$=1 and $T$=3---the two configurations with dedicated weight-scale calibration---deploy successfully (55.05\% and 57.35\% respectively).
All uncalibrated configurations ($T$=2,4,5,7) collapse to $\sim$3--4\%, demonstrating that deployment success depends on per-model hardware calibration, not timestep count.
This finding has practical implications: each new model configuration requires its own SpiNNaker weight-scale sweep, and ``plug-and-play'' deployment of arbitrary SNN models is not currently viable on SpiNNaker~1.

Table~\ref{tab:energy} presents energy from two perspectives.
The top panel uses NeuroBench's sparsity-aware per-sample accounting~\cite{yik2025} (fold~4): the SNN at $T$=3 requires 142~nJ versus 454~nJ for the ANN---\textbf{3.2$\times$ cheaper}---because 5.1$\times$ lower per-AC cost outweighs the higher operation count at moderate spike rates ($\sim$31\%).
At $T$=1, energy drops to just 47~nJ (\textbf{9.6$\times$ cheaper than ANN}), with only 1.3~pp additional accuracy loss compared to $T$=3.

The bottom panel shows how pruning compounds this advantage.
Even under a conservative counting method (total effective ACs, 5-fold mean), 85\% pruning reduces SNN energy 3.5$\times$.
Applying this ratio to the NeuroBench baseline gives an estimated $\sim$41~nJ at $T$=3 with 85\% pruning, though this combines two accounting methods.
At 85\% sparsity, 85\% of weights are zeroed and $\sim$69\% of remaining synapses are silent per timestep---a compounding sparsity that neuromorphic hardware exploits.
The full Pareto frontier (Table~\ref{tab:energy}, bottom) shows that pruning \emph{improves} accuracy up to 60\% sparsity before gradually trading accuracy for energy savings.

\subsection{Temporal Efficiency}

Evaluating the baseline SNN ($T$=25, direct encoding) at reduced timestep budgets ($T \in \{1, 3, 5, 7, 10, 15, 20, 25\}$), accuracy reaches 90\% of its full value at $T$=7, corresponding to a 72\% reduction in total operations.
Performance plateaus around $T$=15--20, with $T$=20 and $T$=25 statistically indistinguishable ($p$=0.45).
This validates the Rhythm SNN's use of $T$=3: the oscillatory threshold modulation compensates for the aggressive temporal truncation, recovering the full $T$=25 accuracy (61.10\% vs 47.15\%) in just 3 steps.
The energy implication is direct: fewer timesteps $=$ proportionally fewer ACs $=$ proportionally lower energy.

Under FGSM~\cite{goodfellow2015} at $\varepsilon$=0.1, the SNN retains 16.55\% accuracy versus 2.75\% for the ANN (\textbf{6.0$\times$ more robust}, $p$=0.007, 5-fold validated; Table~\ref{tab:adversarial}).

The LIF threshold acts as a non-linear filter: small perturbations that shift continuous activations have no effect on the binary output spike until crossing the firing threshold, providing inherent input-level noise rejection.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% ============================================================

\textbf{Why does pruning improve hardware accuracy?}
We identify two complementary mechanisms.
First, pruning removes small-magnitude weights that are most susceptible to integer quantization error.
With fewer weights, the surviving connections have larger magnitudes that quantize more faithfully.
Second, the sparser weight matrix produces cleaner spike patterns: fewer simultaneous synaptic inputs reduce the chance of excitatory-inhibitory cancellation that plagued the full (unpruned) deployment.
This ``pruning as regularization'' effect is consistent with lottery ticket hypothesis findings~\cite{eshraghian2023} but novel in the context of neuromorphic hardware deployment.

\textbf{Negative hardware gaps.}
At 60\% and 80\% pruning, SpiNNaker \emph{exceeds} snnTorch.
We attribute this to beneficial noise injection from integer weight rounding at specific sparsity levels where the weight magnitude distribution interacts favorably with the quantization grid.
This is analogous to stochastic resonance---a phenomenon we verified independently in our SNN (accuracy improves by +0.25~pp with $\sigma$=0.02 noise).

\textbf{FC$_1$ failure as co-design insight.}
Standard conv$\to$AvgPool$\to$FC architectures are not directly deployable on spike-only hardware.
AvgPool produces fractional outputs; FC$_1$ weights with near-zero mean cause cancellation.
The solution---replacing AvgPool with MaxPool---preserves binary outputs and enables full deployment (verified on fold~4: 43.75\% with threshold=3.0).
Full MaxPool-retrained SpiNNaker deployment is future work; our FC$_2$-only hybrid demonstrates the pipeline and quantifies the gap.

\textbf{Practical deployment path.}
In a real system, CNN14 extracts features in software (92.50\% accuracy), then the pruned SNN head classifies on SpiNNaker ($<$1~pp loss, 3.5$\times$ energy reduction).
This hybrid paradigm---heavy feature extraction in software, lightweight spiking classification on neuromorphic hardware---is the emerging standard~\cite{seekings2024}.

\textbf{Connection to the lottery ticket hypothesis.}
Frankle~\&~Carlin~\cite{frankle2019} showed that dense networks contain sparse subnetworks (``winning tickets'') that train to full accuracy.
Our finding extends this to the hardware deployment setting: the pruned FC$_2$ subnetwork is not merely as good as the dense network on SpiNNaker---it is \emph{better}, because the surviving high-magnitude weights quantize more faithfully to SpiNNaker's integer representation.
The ``winning ticket'' for neuromorphic hardware is the subnetwork whose weight distribution is best aligned with the target quantization grid.
This suggests that hardware-aware pruning criteria (e.g., maximizing post-quantization fidelity rather than minimizing magnitude) could further improve deployment accuracy.

\textbf{The gap is feature learning, not spiking.}
The PANNs result (Table~\ref{tab:panns}) is perhaps the most important finding for practical deployment: with frozen AudioSet-pretrained CNN14 features, the SNN achieves 92.50\% versus 93.45\% for the ANN---a gap of just 0.95~pp ($p$=0.034).
The 16.7~pp scratch gap arises from learning convolutional filters from only 1{,}600 samples with approximate surrogate gradients and binary spike bottlenecks~\cite{deng2020}---not from a fundamental limitation of spiking computation.
This points to a practical deployment architecture: a pretrained ANN extracts features in software, and a lightweight SNN classifier runs on neuromorphic hardware at a fraction of the energy cost.

\textbf{Why direct encoding dominates.}
Direct encoding feeds continuous spectrogram values at every timestep, letting the LIF membrane dynamics perform their own temporal integration.
Spike-converted encodings lose information: rate discards magnitude through Poisson sampling, latency collapses temporal richness into a single spike, and delta differentiates an already-static input.
The phase--rate equivalence (24.15\% vs 24.00\%, $p$=0.93) despite 7$\times$ fewer spikes shows that temporal window coverage---not total spike count---determines accuracy.

\textbf{Calibration as deployment bottleneck.}
The timestep sweep (Table~\ref{tab:tsweep}) reveals that deployment success is determined entirely by whether per-model weight-scale calibration was performed, not by the timestep count itself.
Both calibrated configurations ($T$=1 and $T$=3) achieve $<$4~pp hardware gaps; all four uncalibrated configurations collapse to near-chance.
This is a cautionary finding for the neuromorphic community: software-trained SNN weights cannot be naively transferred to SpiNNaker without a hardware-specific calibration step.
The 9-point logarithmic scale sweep we use takes $\sim$5 minutes per model on SpiNNaker; automating this for arbitrary architectures is an important engineering challenge for SpiNNaker~2~\cite{hoppner2024}.

\textbf{Surrogate gradient sensitivity.}
An ablation of seven surrogate gradient functions reveals a bimodal distribution: spike-rate-escape (46.00\%), fast sigmoid (44.75\%), and arctangent (35.75\%) learn successfully, while four others fail ($\leq$10.25\%).
This contrasts with Zenke~\&~Vogels~\cite{zenke2021} who found surrogate shape matters less for image classification.
For audio spectrograms, the surrogate function is a critical hyperparameter.

\textbf{Continual learning advantage.}
Sequential training on ESC-50's five super-categories (10 classes each) reveals that the SNN forgets 4.8~pp less than the ANN (69.9\%$\pm$4.3\% vs 74.7\%$\pm$2.4\%, 5-fold validated).
While both models suffer severe catastrophic forgetting (no replay or regularization), the SNN's sparse binary activations provide a modest but consistent advantage---relevant for always-on audio systems that must adapt to new sound classes over time.

\textbf{Per-category deployment analysis.}
SpiNNaker performance varies systematically across ESC-50's five super-categories.
On fold~4 (400 samples), Natural sounds (rain, sea waves, fire) achieve the highest SpiNNaker accuracy (61.3\%) while Domestic sounds (keyboard, glass breaking) are hardest (31.2\%).
The overall SpiNNaker--snnTorch agreement rate is 64.5\%, but individual classes show extreme variation: airplane benefits from hardware deployment (+37.5~pp over snnTorch) while insects collapses to 0\%.
This suggests that spectral characteristics interact with integer weight quantization: broadband environmental sounds (rain, fire) survive quantization better than transient impulsive sounds (glass breaking, mouse click) that require precise timing information.

\textbf{Limitations.}
(1)~Only FC$_2$ runs on SpiNNaker; full deployment requires replacing AvgPool with MaxPool and retraining (verified feasible: 43.75\% on fold~4 with threshold=3.0).
(2)~Energy is estimated via NeuroBench operation counting, not measured hardware power; actual SpiNNaker power measurement requires on-board metering not available through remote access.
(3)~ESC-50 is small (2{,}000 clips); generalization to UrbanSound8K or FSD50K is untested.
(4)~Adversarial evaluation uses standard PGD; SA-PGD~\cite{wang2025} may reveal different robustness profiles.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We present the first convolutional SNN evaluation on ESC-50, with a Rhythm SNN achieving 61.10\%---only 2.75~pp below the ANN---and deploy pruned models on SpiNNaker neuromorphic hardware.
The key finding is that \textbf{pruning acts as implicit regularization for hardware deployment}: 50\% sparsity improves SpiNNaker accuracy by 3.1~pp while reducing energy, and at 85\% sparsity the hardware gap is only 0.6~pp with 3.5$\times$ energy savings.
At two sparsity levels, SpiNNaker exceeds its own software simulation.
These 22{,}000 hardware inferences across 10 pruning levels constitute the most comprehensive neuromorphic audio deployment study to date.

Several directions emerge from this work.
First, SpiNNaker~2~\cite{hoppner2024} deployment with MaxPool-retrained architectures would enable full on-chip inference, eliminating the software feature-extraction step.
Second, structured pruning (removing entire neurons rather than individual weights) could exploit SpiNNaker's core-level parallelism for additional energy savings.
Third, cross-dataset validation on UrbanSound8K and FSD50K would test generalization beyond ESC-50.
Fourth, combining pruning with the PANNs+SNN hybrid (92.50\% accuracy) could yield a high-accuracy, energy-efficient system suitable for real-world always-on audio monitoring.
Finally, measuring actual SpiNNaker power consumption (rather than NeuroBench estimates) would provide ground-truth energy numbers for the pruned deployment pipeline.
