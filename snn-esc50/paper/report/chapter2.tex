\section{Environmental Sound Classification}
\label{sec:esc}
%=================================================================

Environmental sound classification (ESC) is a core task within computational auditory scene analysis, requiring a system to assign a categorical label to a short audio recording of a real-world acoustic event~\citep{barchiesi2015}. Applications include noise pollution monitoring in smart cities~\citep{bello2019}, automated wildlife census~\citep{stowell2019}, assistive technology for the hearing-impaired, and anomaly detection in industrial settings. Unlike speech recognition, ESC must handle the full diversity of non-linguistic acoustic events---from a dog barking to a chainsaw operating---without the benefit of structured phonological constraints.

\paragraph{The ESC-50 dataset.}
\gls{esc}~\citep{piczak2015} is the standard benchmark for this task. It comprises 2{,}000 five-second recordings drawn from the Freesound repository, distributed uniformly across 50 classes (40 clips per class). The classes span five broad super-categories: animals (e.g.\ dog, rooster, crow), natural soundscapes (e.g.\ rain, sea waves, thunderstorm), human non-speech sounds (e.g.\ clapping, coughing, laughing), domestic/indoor sounds (e.g.\ clock alarm, door knock, vacuum cleaner), and urban/outdoor sounds (e.g.\ helicopter, siren, car horn). Crucially, the dataset defines five predefined folds for cross-validation, ensuring that results are directly comparable across studies. Human classification performance on ESC-50 is 81.3\%~\citep{piczak2015}, reflecting the genuine perceptual difficulty of some distinctions (e.g.\ sea waves versus rain, insects versus crackling fire).

The current state of the art is 99.1\%, achieved by OmniVec2~\citep{srivastava2024} using a multi-modal Transformer pretrained on diverse data. Among audio-only methods, Pretrained Audio Neural Networks~(\gls{panns}) with CNN14 fine-tuning achieve 94.7\%~\citep{kong2020}, and Audio Spectrogram Transformers reach 95.6\%~\citep{gong2021}. These results rely on large-scale pretraining on AudioSet~\citep{gemmeke2017}, a dataset of approximately 2 million 10-second clips spanning 527 event categories. Without external pretraining, the best reported \gls{ann} performance on ESC-50 is substantially lower, motivating our choice to evaluate scratch-trained models as the primary comparison.

\paragraph{Input representation.}
The dominant input representation for ESC is the log-mel spectrogram, computed by applying the short-time Fourier transform to the audio waveform, mapping the resulting frequency bins through a bank of triangular mel-scale filters that approximate the frequency resolution of the human cochlea, and converting power to a logarithmic decibel scale. In this work, we extract spectrograms with 64 mel bands at a sampling rate of 22{,}050\,Hz and a hop length of 512 samples, yielding a representation of shape $(1, 64, 216)$ per clip---one channel, 64 frequency bins, and 216 time frames. Values are normalised to $[0, 1]$ per sample, a requirement for the spike encoding methods described in Section~\ref{subsec:encoding}.

\paragraph{Broader context.}
Other datasets complement ESC-50. UrbanSound8K~\citep{salamon2014} provides 8{,}732 clips across 10 urban sound classes; it is smaller in vocabulary and widely considered less challenging. AudioSet~\citep{gemmeke2017} provides massive scale (527 classes, 2 million clips) but uses weak labels and variable-quality recordings, making it unsuitable as a controlled evaluation benchmark. The Spiking Heidelberg Digits (SHD) and Spiking Speech Commands datasets~\citep{cramer2020} are natively spiking but address spoken-digit recognition rather than environmental sounds. No standard spiking benchmark exists for ESC, underscoring the need for this work.

\section{Spiking Neural Networks}
\label{sec:snn-background}
%=================================================================

\Glspl{snn} are neural networks in which information is communicated through discrete, binary events called spikes. Unlike conventional \glspl{ann}, where neurons transmit continuous-valued activations, \gls{snn} neurons accumulate incoming signals over time in a membrane potential and emit a spike only when that potential exceeds a threshold. This event-driven, temporal computation paradigm is inspired by biological neural circuits and offers potential advantages in energy efficiency and temporal processing~\citep{maass1997,gerstner2002}.

%-------------------------------
\subsection{The Leaky Integrate-and-Fire Neuron}
\label{subsec:lif}
%-------------------------------

The \gls{lif} neuron is the most widely used model in modern \gls{snn} research, balancing biological plausibility with computational tractability~\citep{gerstner2002}. Its continuous-time dynamics describe an RC circuit with passive membrane leakage:

\begin{definition}[Leaky Integrate-and-Fire Neuron]
\label{def:lif}
The membrane potential $U(t)$ of a \gls{lif} neuron evolves according to the differential equation
\begin{equation}
    \tau_m \frac{dU(t)}{dt} = -U(t) + R \, I(t),
    \label{eq:lif-continuous}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_m$ is the membrane time constant, $R$ is the membrane resistance, and $I(t)$ is the input current. When $U(t)$ reaches the firing threshold $\vartheta$, the neuron emits a spike and the membrane potential resets:
\begin{equation}
    S(t) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } U(t) \geq \vartheta, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases}
    \qquad
    U(t^+) = U_{\mathrm{reset}} \quad \text{if } S(t) = 1.
    \label{eq:lif-spike}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

For simulation in discrete timesteps of duration $\Delta t$, as required by GPU-based training frameworks, the continuous dynamics yield the recurrence:
\begin{equation}
    U[t+1] = \beta \cdot U[t] + W \cdot X[t] - S[t] \cdot \vartheta,
    \label{eq:lif-discrete}
\end{equation}
where $\beta = \exp(-\Delta t / \tau_m)$ is the decay factor, $W$ is the weight matrix, $X[t]$ is the input at timestep $t$, and the final term implements the post-spike reset by subtracting the threshold. The spike output is
\begin{equation}
    S[t] = \mathbf{1}\bigl[U[t] \geq \vartheta\bigr],
    \label{eq:spike-function}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{1}[\cdot]$ is the Heaviside step function. In this work, we set $\beta = 0.95$ (corresponding to $\tau_m \approx 20$\,ms at $\Delta t = 1$\,ms) and $\vartheta = 1.0$ across all layers, following the snnTorch framework defaults~\citep{eshraghian2023}.

The \gls{lif} model captures two essential properties of biological neurons that are absent from standard \gls{ann} units: \emph{temporal integration}, where evidence accumulates over multiple timesteps before a decision (spike) is made; and \emph{leaky memory}, where past inputs are exponentially forgotten, preventing indefinite charge build-up. These properties are central to the robustness advantages discussed in Section~\ref{sec:adversarial-cl}.

%-------------------------------
\subsection{Surrogate Gradient Training}
\label{subsec:surrogate}
%-------------------------------

The spike function in Equation~\eqref{eq:spike-function} is a Heaviside step, which has a derivative of zero almost everywhere and is undefined at the threshold. This renders standard backpropagation through time (BPTT) inapplicable, since the gradient $\partial S / \partial U$ vanishes for all sub-threshold and supra-threshold membrane potentials. For much of the history of \gls{snn} research, this non-differentiability confined learning to biologically inspired rules such as spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP)~\citep{bi1998}, which are local and unsupervised, and which do not scale to complex classification tasks.

The surrogate gradient method~\citep{neftci2019,zenke2021} resolves this by decoupling the forward and backward passes. The forward pass uses exact binary spikes as in Equation~\eqref{eq:spike-function}, preserving the discrete communication that underpins the energy argument. The backward pass substitutes a smooth, differentiable surrogate $\sigma(U - \vartheta)$ in place of the Heaviside:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial U} \approx \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial S} \cdot \sigma'(U - \vartheta),
    \label{eq:surrogate-gradient}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma'$ is the derivative of the chosen surrogate function. This enables full BPTT through the unrolled spiking network, using the same optimisers, loss functions, and learning rate schedules as conventional deep learning.

Several surrogate functions have been proposed. The \emph{fast sigmoid} surrogate, used as the default in snnTorch~\citep{eshraghian2023}, is defined as $\sigma'(x) = 1 / (1 + k|x|)^2$ with slope parameter $k = 25$. The \emph{spike rate escape} surrogate uses an exponential form $\sigma'(x) = (1/\beta_s) \exp(-|x|/\beta_s)$, motivated by the theory of escape noise in stochastic neuron models~\citep{gerstner2002,gygax2025}. Other common choices include the arctangent, triangular, and straight-through estimator (STE) surrogates.

\citet{zenke2021} argued that the precise shape of the surrogate matters less than its slope, since all smooth approximations to the Heaviside convey similar gradient information. However, our ablation study in Chapter~\ref{ch:results-core} challenges this claim: across eight surrogates evaluated under identical conditions, we observe a bimodal distribution in which three surrogates (spike rate escape, fast sigmoid, arctangent) achieve 35--46\% accuracy while the remaining five (STE, sigmoid, sfs, triangular, and the stochastic variant) either fail to converge or collapse to chance performance. This suggests that surrogate selection is a consequential design decision, particularly for small-dataset regimes where training stability is fragile.

\subsection{Spike Encoding Methods}
\label{subsec:encoding}
%-------------------------------

Spike encoding is the process of converting continuous-valued inputs---in our case, mel spectrogram pixel intensities in $[0, 1]$---into temporal spike patterns suitable for \gls{snn} processing. The choice of encoding determines the information content per spike, the temporal structure of input activity, and the resulting energy cost. Seven methods are evaluated in this work, spanning the major categories identified in the computational neuroscience literature.

\paragraph{Rate coding~\citep{adrian1926}.}
Each input neuron emits a spike at each of the $T$ timesteps with probability equal to the input intensity:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{spk}[t, i] \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(x_i), \quad t = 1, \ldots, T.
    \label{eq:rate-coding}
\end{equation}
Rate coding is the oldest and most studied encoding in both neuroscience and \gls{snn} research. It provides maximal information content---$\mathcal{O}(T)$ bits per neuron---at the expense of high spike counts and corresponding energy cost. The stochastic nature introduces trial-to-trial variability, which can act as implicit regularisation but also limits reproducibility.

\paragraph{Latency coding~\citep{thorpe1996}.}
Each neuron fires at most once, with the spike time inversely related to input intensity:
\begin{equation}
    t_{\mathrm{fire}}(x_i) = \bigl\lceil (1 - x_i) \cdot (T - 1) \bigr\rceil.
    \label{eq:latency-coding}
\end{equation}
High-intensity inputs spike early, low-intensity inputs spike late or not at all. Information content is $\mathcal{O}(\log T)$ bits per neuron, and the single-spike constraint makes latency coding maximally energy-efficient. However, downstream \gls{lif} neurons must integrate over many timesteps before the late-arriving spikes contribute, creating a temporal bottleneck.

\paragraph{Delta coding~\citep{lichtsteiner2008}.}
Spikes encode temporal changes rather than absolute values:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{spk}[t, i] = \mathbf{1}\bigl[|x_i[t] - x_i[t-1]| > \delta\bigr],
    \label{eq:delta-coding}
\end{equation}
where $\delta$ is a threshold (set to 0.1 in this work). Delta coding is the computational analogue of dynamic vision sensors and retinal on/off cells. It is well suited to signals with rich temporal dynamics but performs poorly on static inputs: since a mel spectrogram is identical across all $T$ timesteps in the absence of temporal perturbation, delta coding produces near-zero spike activity, depriving downstream layers of information.

\paragraph{Direct (continuous) coding~\citep{rathi2021}.}
The normalised input is injected as a continuous current at every timestep:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{input}[t, i] = x_i, \quad \forall \, t = 1, \ldots, T.
    \label{eq:direct-coding}
\end{equation}
