\chapter{Methodology}
\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter describes the experimental design, dataset preprocessing, network architecture, spike encoding implementations, training protocol, SpiNNaker deployment pipeline, energy measurement framework, and advanced evaluation methods used throughout this work. Every design decision is motivated by the principle of controlled comparison: the SNN and ANN differ \emph{only} in their neuron model, so that observed performance differences are attributable to the spiking formalism rather than to confounding architectural or training factors.

% ==================================================================
\section{Design Philosophy}
\label{sec:design-philosophy}

The central experimental principle is \textbf{controlled comparison}. Both the \gls{snn} and \gls{ann} share identical convolutional feature extraction layers, identical fully connected classifier dimensions, and identical training hyperparameters. They are trained with the same optimiser, learning rate schedule, early stopping criterion, and evaluated under the same five-fold cross-validation protocol. The sole difference is the neuron activation: the SNN uses \gls{lif} neurons with surrogate gradient training~\citep{neftci2019surrogate}, whereas the ANN uses ReLU activations with standard backpropagation.

This design follows the recommendation of \citet{deng2021optimal} for fair SNN--ANN comparison and ensures that any accuracy gap can be attributed to the spiking formalism and spike encoding scheme, not to differences in model capacity, regularisation, or data exposure. The parameter count is deliberately kept small ($\sim$622K) to avoid overfitting on the 1,600 training samples available per fold in ESC-50, and to remain deployable on SpiNNaker hardware within its per-core memory constraints.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:dataset}

\paragraph{ESC-50.}
ESC-50~\citep{piczak2015esc} comprises 2,000 five-second environmental sound recordings drawn from Freesound.org, evenly distributed across 50 classes with 40 clips per class. The classes are grouped into five broad super-categories: \emph{Animals} (classes 0--9), \emph{Natural soundscapes} (10--19), \emph{Human non-speech sounds} (20--29), \emph{Domestic sounds} (30--39), and \emph{Urban noises} (40--49). The dataset provides five predefined cross-validation folds that are balanced by class and avoid speaker or recording overlap across folds; we use these throughout. Human classification accuracy on ESC-50 is 81.3\%~\citep{piczak2015esc}.

\paragraph{Preprocessing.}
All audio clips are loaded at a sample rate of 22,050\,Hz and zero-padded to exactly 110,250 samples (5.0\,s). A log-mel spectrogram is then computed:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mel}
\mathbf{S} = 10 \cdot \log_{10}\!\bigl(\text{MelFilterbank}(|\text{STFT}(y)|^2) \,/\, S_{\max}\bigr),
\end{equation}
where the STFT uses $n_\text{fft} = 1024$ and hop length $= 512$, and the mel filterbank has $n_\text{mels} = 64$ bins spanning the full frequency range (0--11,025\,Hz). The resulting spectrogram has shape $(64 \times 216)$.

Each spectrogram is then min--max normalised per sample:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:norm}
\hat{s}_{ij} = \frac{s_{ij} - \min(\mathbf{S})}{\max(\mathbf{S}) - \min(\mathbf{S})},
\end{equation}
yielding values in $[0, 1]$. This normalisation is essential for spike encoding: rate coding generates spikes with probability equal to pixel intensity, latency coding maps intensity to spike time, and direct coding feeds values as membrane current---all require a bounded $[0, 1]$ input range. A channel dimension is prepended to produce the final input tensor of shape $(1, 64, 216)$.

All 2,000 spectrograms are precomputed and cached in memory prior to training. This eliminates I/O overhead and ensures deterministic data loading with \texttt{librosa}~\citep{mcfee2015librosa}.
